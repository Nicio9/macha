CS345: Computability and Automata Theory

Semester: 2nd Semester
Credits: 16
Year: 3rd Year
Prerequisites: CS214

Course Description

This course introduces the mathematical foundations of computer science through the study of formal languages, automata, and computability. Students explore the theoretical limits of what computers can and cannot compute, and the relationship between different classes of computational problems.

The course is central to the CS curriculum at Stellenbosch University, where the department has a distinguished research tradition in automata theory. Students will develop rigorous mathematical proof skills alongside deep insight into the nature of computation.

Topics Covered

  • Finite automata: deterministic (DFA) and non-deterministic (NFA)
  • Regular languages and regular expressions
  • Closure properties and the Pumping Lemma for regular languages
  • Pushdown automata (PDA)
  • Context-free grammars (CFG) and context-free languages
  • The Pumping Lemma for context-free languages
  • Turing machines: definition, variants, and Church–Turing thesis
  • Decidability: decidable and undecidable problems
  • The Halting Problem and reductions
  • Introduction to computational complexity: P, NP, NP-completeness

Learning Outcomes

  • Construct and reason about finite automata and regular languages
  • Design context-free grammars and pushdown automata
  • Apply the Pumping Lemmas to prove languages are not regular or not context-free
  • Describe the Turing machine model of computation and its variants
  • Demonstrate the undecidability of the Halting Problem using diagonalisation and reductions
  • Distinguish between the complexity classes P and NP and identify NP-complete problems

Assessment

  • Problem sets and assignments (30%)
  • Written tests (30%)
  • Final examination (40%)

Recommended Textbook

Sipser, M. (2012). Introduction to the Theory of Computation (3rd ed.). Cengage Learning.
